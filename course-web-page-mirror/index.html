Course Description

This course is to introduce students to the basic concepts and techniques in the theory of computation. Students will learn the basic concepts, proofs and techniques in automata, computability and complexity theory. The syllabus includes the following:
  • Basics: sets, sequences, tuples, functions, relations and graphs.
  • Automata theory: DFA, NFA, regular and nonregular languages, pumping lemma.
  • Context-free and non-context-free languages, PDA, pumping lemma.
  • Computability Theory: Turing machines, decidable and undecidable problems, the Halting problem, reducibility.
  • Complexity theory: P, NP, NP-complete.

Textbook

  • Introduction to the Theory of Computation, Michael Sipser, Third Edition.

Course Outcomes

At the end of the course, students should be able to:

  • Understand the elementary concepts, proofs and techniques on finite state automata and regular languages.
  • Understand the elementary concepts, proofs and techniques on pushdown automata and context-free languages.
  • Understand the elementary concepts, proofs and techniques on computability theory. Be able to do simple mapping reductions.
  • Understand the elementary concepts, proofs and techniques on complexity theory and NP-completeness. Be able to do simple polynomial reductions.
  • Know how to handle intractable problems with heuristic methods.

Grading

This a theory course. The majority of your grade comes from assignments, tests, and a final. Due to Covid-19, there will be no participation counting.
    6 Assignments (the worst replaced by the best)            ======> 42%
    3 In-class Tests (announced at least one week ahead)    =====> 30%
    Final                                    =========================> 22%
    Best Exam Grade (Best of the 3 tests and the final)    =======> 6%
    Quizzes (lightly graded, best 6 of 7, starting March 1)    =====> 6%

Each of the 3 in-class tests will be announced at least one week ahead.

The final is optional. If you are happy with your 3 tests, the average of your 3 tests (in percentage) multiply 22 will be your final score. If not, you can choose to take the optional final. (The optional final cannot be taken early.) The Best Exam Grade is counted for the best of your 3 tests and the final; for instance, the scores of your 3 tests and the final are 75%, 80%, 70% and 65% respectively, then you get 80% of these 6 points, which is 4.8. Starting March 1, 7 quizzes will be given (the best 6 will be counted). Each quizz will be given 2-3 days to finish and will be (lightly) graded. On the other hand, note that a quiz is not an attendance counting and you have to spend some time to work on it.

At the end of the semester, final grades will be determined based on your overall performance on assignments, tests and the final exam.

Extra credit/chance will be possible after the 3 tests are all over, and details will be posted then. On the other hand, any such chance/curving will be fairly applied to all students.

Late Policy

Assignments will be submitted in .pdf format through D2L, and no late assignment is accepted. Exception will only be given to extreme cases, like a student being seriously sick. It is strongly recommended that you use Latex to generate .pdf files for your assignments; in fact, for Assignment 1, it is mandatory that you use Latex to finish it. In the first lecture, I will show a quick demo on how to use the template to generate a .pdf file.

Latex Usage

  • you can create an account on Overleaf, which is free (as long as you share your project to at most one collaborator).

Collaboration Policy

You have to finish your assignment alone, unless it is said clearly that the corresponding assignment could be a group assignment (i.e., a group of at most 3 students submit a solution, and all the 3 get the same grade for that assignment).

Even for a non-group assignment, you still may (unless otherwise noted)

  • Discuss your ideas with other people (including on D2L), but still have to write your solution by yourself.
  • Use Internet resources, provided that a reference is given.

Failure to abide by these rules will result in everyone involved being reported to the Dean of Students and receiving an F for the course.
